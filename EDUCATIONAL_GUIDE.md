# Educational Guide: Span of Attention Using Tachistoscope

## 📚 Course Overview

This guide helps students and educators understand the psychological principles behind the Span of Attention experiment and how to use the virtual lab effectively.

## 🧠 Psychology Foundations

### What is Attention?

Attention is the cognitive process of selectively focusing on specific information while filtering out irrelevant stimuli. It's a limited resource - your brain cannot consciously process all available information at once.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Selective**: Choose what to focus on
- **Limited**: Can only process finite amount of information
- **Dynamic**: Can shift and change over time
- **Measurable**: Can be tested and quantified

### Span of Attention (Attentional Span)

The **span of attention** refers to the maximum number of separate items that can be clearly perceived and reported after a single, brief exposure.

**Historical Context:**
- **George Miller (1956)**: Proposed "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two"
- Suggests humans can typically hold 5-9 items in working memory
- Revolutionary study changed how we understand cognitive limits

**Related Concept: Working Memory**
- The cognitive system for temporarily holding and manipulating information
- Capacity: approximately 7 ± 2 items for most people
- Duration: about 10-30 seconds without rehearsal

## 🔬 The Tachistoscope: Historical Background

### Origins
The tachistoscope was developed in the 19th century as a precision optical instrument for presenting visual stimuli for very brief durations.

**Original Purpose in Psychology:**
- Study perception and attention
- Measure reaction times
- Research visual memory
- Explore subliminal perception

### How It Works
The classic tachistoscope uses:
1. A light source
2. Optical shutters
3. A viewing screen
4. Precisely timed exposure (typically 10-1000 milliseconds)

### Digital Adaptation
This virtual lab replicates the classic methodology using:
- Digital display
- Programmed timing
- Instant response recording
- Automated analysis

## 📊 Experimental Procedure

### Step-by-Step Methodology

#### Phase 1: Stimulus Preparation
- Select stimulus type (letters, numbers, shapes, words)
- Arrange items in random order
- Ensure items are familiar to participants
- Prepare 10-20 practice-trial combinations

#### Phase 2: Presentation
- Display stimulus on tachistoscope for set duration
- Exposure time: 0.1 - 1.0 seconds
- Remove stimulus suddenly
- No opportunity to "look around"

#### Phase 3: Response
- Participant reports items perceived
- Can be in any order
- Record verbatim response
- Measure response time

#### Phase 4: Analysis
- Count correct items identified
- Calculate accuracy percentage
- Track average span across trials
- Note patterns and improvements

### Variables in the Experiment

#### Independent Variables (What We Change)
1. **Exposure Duration**
   - 0.1 seconds (very brief)
   - 0.3 seconds (quick)
   - 0.5 seconds (moderate)
   - 1.0 seconds (extended)

2. **Stimulus Type**
   - Familiar letters (A, B, C)
   - Numbers (1, 2, 3)
   - Shapes (▲ ● ■)
   - Words (CAT, DOG, SUN)
   - Mixed items

3. **Number of Items**
   - Usually 3-10 items per trial
   - Increases complexity

#### Dependent Variables (What We Measure)
1. **Accuracy**: Percentage of correct items recalled
2. **Span**: Number of items correctly identified
3. **Reaction Time**: Time to respond
4. **Success Rate**: Trials with 100% accuracy

## 📋 Expected Results

### Typical Findings

**Span Range**: Most people show a span of **5-7 items**

**Effect of Exposure Time:**
- 0.1s: Lower accuracy (30-50%)
- 0.3s: Moderate accuracy (60-75%)
- 0.5s: High accuracy (80-90%)
- 1.0s: Very high accuracy (90%+)

**Effect of Stimulus Type:**
- Letters: High accuracy (familiar symbols)
- Numbers: High accuracy (familiar symbols)
- Shapes: Moderate accuracy (encoding takes time)
- Words: Varies (depends on word familiarity)
- Mixed: Lower accuracy (diverse encoding required)

**Learning Effect:**
- Performance often improves across trials
- Practice effect: 10-20% improvement possible
- Plateaus after 15-20 trials

## 🔍 Factors Affecting Span of Attention

### Facilitating Factors (Improve Span)

1. **Familiarity**
   - Familiar items are processed faster
   - Student: Using letters vs. Chinese characters

2. **Chunking**
   - Grouping related items together
   - Example: "357" vs. "3 5 7"
   - Phone numbers are easier as chunked units

3. **Concentration**
   - Alert, focused state increases span
   - Fatigue or distraction reduces span

4. **Expectation**
   - Anticipating stimulus helps
   - Knowledge of possible items aids recall

5. **Motivation**
   - Goal-directed attention is stronger
   - Incentives improve performance

### Interfering Factors (Reduce Span)

1. **Unfamiliarity**
   - Novel or random items harder to process
   - Longer encoding time needed

2. **Complexity**
   - More complex items take longer to process
   - Shapes may be harder than letters

3. **Fatigue**
   - Attention degrades with fatigue
   - Mental effort is required

4. **Distraction**
   - External stimuli divert attention
   - Internal thoughts disrupt focus

5. **Anxiety**
   - Stress narrows attentional focus
   - Test anxiety Can impair performance

## 📈 Data Analysis

### Calculating Key Metrics

**Accuracy Percentage:**
```
Accuracy = (Correct Items / Total Items) × 100
```

**Average Span:**
```
Avg Span = Total Correct Items / Number of Trials
```

**Success Rate:**
```
Success Rate = Trials with 100% Accuracy / Total Trials × 100
```

**Reaction Time:**
```
Avg RT = Sum of All Reaction Times / Number of Trials
```

### Statistical Considerations

1. **Sample Size**: Need 10+ trials for reliable estimates
2. **Validity**: Ensure participants understand instructions
3. **Reliability**: Repeated testing shows consistency
4. **Confounds**: Control for fatigue, learning, distraction
